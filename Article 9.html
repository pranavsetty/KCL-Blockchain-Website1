Blockchain-Based Solutions to Tackle the Age-old Problem of Scattered Medical Records

March 17, 2018

Charmaine Mabarana

In a world where fast paced developments are occurring, the scattered management of medical data records seems to be an archaic problem that has been left behind in the whirlwind of technological advancements.

The problem of patient medical records is based on the fact that different healthcare providers use different databases and systems to store patient records. There is no single integrated database between GPs, hospitals, and other healthcare providers on a national scale, let alone on a global scale as of yet. This is where the decentralised immutable aspects of Blockchain technology can be utilised to offer a solution to a global problem. Several companies have recognised this opportunity and have been steadily developing platforms which focus on improving the landscape of big data management in the medical sector.

A prominent example of one such company is London-based Medicalchain, who raised over $24m in their ICO in February 2018. Following their successful ICO and backing from the Coinsillium accelerator, hyperledger, the NHS as well as a partnership with The Groves Medical Group for a pilot project in July 2018, the company is certainly setting a trend for a complete overhaul in healthcare data management.

In addition to decentralising medical records, companies are looking into how patients can gain control of their own data, have access to it when and where they want in the form of a Dapp. This idea has already been conceptualised by Proof Work who introduced it at the Disrupt Berlin Startup Battlefield 2017. They aim to allow patients to contribute or sell their anonymised data, stored in their Decentralised Electronic Medical Record (DEMR) via contractual agreements with their health care provider.

From a financial perspective, patient autonomy over data, coupled with a cryptocurrency linked to patient data sharing, could allow insurance companies to create incentives for patients to carry out certain tasks, such as tracking their blood pressure in return for payment with their coin. Proof Work aims to profit by taking a small percentage from data transfers/incentive based transactions made through their platforms.

However, these projects do have obstacles to surpass. With previous efforts for health data tracking such as Microsoft HealthVault being pulled from the market, it highlights the need for sustainability in this sector. It is hoped that with the incentive of coin renumeration, patients will be more driven to maintain and share their records.

In particular, the risk of data theft and hacking remains a top concern. In order to be viable and trusted, companies will have to comply to with HIPAA and GDPR privacy regulations. ProofWork for example have gained these approvals. Moreover, the highly sensitive nature of patient data and the idea of opening these records up, making them readily available for public use, is a highly controversial matter implicating ethical and privacy concerns.

Designing a democratised system through the blockchain platform that allows individuals to self govern their data, and in the process profit and contribute to healthcare research seems like a holy grail project. With investments pouring into medical based blockchain ventures as well as the healthcare technology sector, access to unified decentralised medical records, autonomy over our medical data and even application of artificial intelligence to tailor healthcare services will all be within our reach, perhaps sooner than we expect.
