Blockchain's digital identities: Reclaiming your right to privacy

December 14, 2017 | Beatrice Zanardi

Blockchain was first created to track bitcoin ownership, but since then it has been applied within various use cases to track pretty much anything that represents value. Through the distributed ledger concept, blockchain solutions offer the promise of a record that is immutable and therefore can be trusted. One of the non-financial use cases that has started to gather most traction by leveraging this technology have been identity applications such as IDs, passports, online account log-ins, birth certificates…etc. Applications range from offering greater peace of mind to individuals regarding the privacy of and control over their identity to empowering developing countries' citizens with greater access to human rights and the global economy through digital citizenship.

Governments and corporations have been granting passports, driving licenses and social security numbers and blessing them with identity representation for centuries. The problem with this physical system of identification is that as easily as it is created, a personal identity can be revoked by your government or invalidated if you are in another country where such ID is not recognised. In general, we are being forced to give a variety of information about ourselves to prove that we are who we say we are when crossing a border, accessing an online service or buying a drink. The approach here is that if our identity were on the blockchain we would have more control over it, presenting the minimum information needed for a specific identification, having a greater control of our privacy and being less vulnerable to hacks.

It might seem trivial, but do you ever wonder why a bouncer at a club should be able to see your name and nationality while checking for your age? – Apply this type of reasoning to an ever-growing digital world in which all your data (medical records, pictures, conversations…) is in hackable format and is being guarded as part of your identity by third parties, be it Facebook, Equifax, or the bouncer that knows your name and nationality apart from your age, and you will quickly recognise the need for self-sovereign identity.

Jerry Cuomo, IBM's VP of Blockchain Technologies, explains the concept of self-sovereign identity: 'Imagine a world in which you always have peace of mind that your personal information is safe. Imagine a world in which your information cannot be shared without your clear, explicit consent at the time of the transaction; where you decide who can access what information, when, and for how long. In this world, you can even later choose to revoke that privilege. You are in control. Every person, organisation, or thing can have its own truly-independent digital identity that no other person, company, or government can take away.'

Well, this vision is already taking place in the city of Zug, Switzerland, also known as Crypto Valley due to its high dedication to cryptocurrencies, where the first digital Zug ID was registered over the Ethereum blockchain last month. This will allow this city to unlock a range of government eServices like online voting and proof of residency, promoting greater trust between government agencies and citizens.

Another example of moving towards a more private and controlled identity is being demonstrated by applications like Civic, who gives users the chance to create an account which collects various identifying information for them, verifies it with trusted third parties (e.g. governments), takes a cryptographic hash of all that information, inserts it into the public blockchain and erases the personal data from their servers. Whenever you need to identify yourself online with a service that is partnered with Civic, you will only give the necessary details for identification and this service will check it against the hash on the blockchain. All of this to say you will have given only the necessary information to identify yourself, no extra details like an email, and no one will need to store that information in their servers, keeping your identity safe and private.

Apart from securing identity and managing it in a more privacy friendly manner, blockchain applications within this realm are starting to have a significant impact within creating verifiable identities for those who do not have a clear identification method due to humanitarian crisis situations. A clear example of this has been the use of blockchain by the Finnish government to help refugees from the Syrian crisis get on their feet faster. For the last two years the Finnish Immigration Service has been giving refugees without bank accounts prepaid Mastercards developed by MONI, a local start up. This card is linked to a unique digital identity stored on a blockchain, and therefore it can be used as a validated bank account, which until now was the main obstacle for asylum seekers missing a strongly authenticated identity to find a job and pay bills.

The UN is also getting involved with blockchain technology to solve various issues under the 2030 Sustainable Development Goal of providing legal identity for everyone on the planet. To date 1.1 billion worldwide live with no official identification, and the UN has teamed up with Accenture and Microsoft to start applying blockchain solutions, like the ones tested in Zug, to help individuals such as refugees prove who they are and gain access to basic services such as healthcare, education and financial services. Another example of the UN's involvement in blockchain based digital ID promotion has been to specifically tackle the increasing sex trafficking and forced labour rates in Moldova, where rural children with no official identification documents are at high risk of trafficking.

Blockchain is taking the world by storm this year with most of the attention being directed towards Bitcoin as an investment opportunity, but this technology's use cases are much more than a cryptocurrency. Digital identity is one of the clearer opportunities that will continue to evolve over blockchain and has the chance to revolutionise the way data is treated, bringing back the power of privacy to individuals and generating an opportunity to connect to the global economy to over 1 billion unbanked people.
